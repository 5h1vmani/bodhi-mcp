# B2B Collateral Design

> Visual design decisions for brochures, one-pagers, and datasheets — what layout, typography, detail level, and psychological framing to use for which audience and funnel stage.

## TL;DR

- **70% of B2B sales collateral goes unused** — stage-mismatch and generic design are the top reasons; create 3 tiers (awareness/consideration/decision), not one brochure for all
- **Lead with emotion, support with data** — creative messaging outperforms purely rational messaging in B2B (Baack et al.); buyers are human even when buying for companies
- **92% of buyers start with a vendor in mind; first on the shortlist wins 80%** — your collateral's job is to get on that list before sales contact, not to close
- **"9 out of 10" beats "90%"** — Cialdini's research shows "out of" framing creates personal connection; percentages feel abstract in print
- **67% of B2B buyers prefer short-form content** (Demand Gen 2024) — one-pagers outperform brochures for consideration stage; save depth for datasheets

## Decision Guide

| Scenario                                   | Approach                                                                                                            | Why                                                                                                    |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| C-suite one-pager (enterprise)             | Z-pattern layout; serif headings; 30% text / 70% whitespace + visuals; one outcome metric above the fold            | Execs scan in <30 seconds; serif signals authority; low cognitive load = trust                         |
| Technical datasheet (engineers/IT)         | F-pattern; clean sans-serif; dense but chunked into labeled sections; include specs, integrations, architecture     | Engineers read deeply but scan structure first; specificity = credibility with technical buyers        |
| Awareness-stage thought piece              | 80/20 education vs product mention; no pricing; data-heavy with shareable stats; brand-subtle                       | 89% of buyers self-educate before engaging sales; 51% find content "too generic" — add research value  |
| Decision-stage proposal insert             | Full specs + ROI calculation + tiered pricing (show premium first) + case study results with named customer         | Buyers consume 3-5 pieces before contacting sales; anchoring on premium makes mid-tier feel reasonable |
| Event/trade show brochure (mixed audience) | Progressive disclosure: cover = hook, inside = detail, back = CTA + contact; Z-pattern per spread                   | Different readers go to different depths; cover is a 2-second qualifying decision                      |
| Competitive comparison piece               | Side-by-side table; "9 out of 10" social proof; third-party validation badges; your advantage column in brand color | Anchoring frames the comparison; authority signals (analyst quotes) override feature parity            |
| Product-led growth leave-behind            | Minimal text; QR to interactive demo; single stat + single testimonial + single CTA                                 | PLG buyers self-serve; print's job is to bridge to digital experience, not to sell                     |

## Pricing Disclosure on Collateral

This is a design decision separate from pricing strategy (see `gtm-pricing-packaging.md`).

| Scenario                                    | Show Price?                               | Why                                                                          |
| ------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Competitive price point, self-serve product | Yes — tiered table, premium tier first    | Transparency qualifies leads; anchoring on premium lifts mid-tier selection  |
| Premium/high-cost enterprise solution       | No — use "Talk to us" or ROI framing      | Price without context triggers sticker shock; value must precede cost        |
| Custom pricing per client                   | No — show "starting at" or ranges only    | Exact pricing misleads; ranges set expectations without committing           |
| Event handout (broad audience)              | No — feature an ROI stat instead          | Unknown audience mix; price on paper filters before you can contextualize    |
| Decision-stage proposal                     | Yes — with TCO framing and ROI calculator | At this stage, withholding price creates friction and signals low confidence |

**B2B pricing note:** Round numbers outperform charm pricing in enterprise. $2,000 signals confidence; $1,997 signals discount-seeking.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake                                        | Fix                                                                                                   |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Feature dump without benefit framing           | Every feature gets "so you can..." or "which means..." (benefit → outcome chain)                      |
| Same brochure for awareness AND decision stage | Create 3 tiers: educational (no sell), comparative (soft sell), detailed (direct sell)                |
| Stock photography / generic business visuals   | Custom illustration or real team/product photos; stock erodes trust (see `trust-building.md`)         |
| Pricing on awareness-stage materials           | Withhold until value is established; early price without context anchors negatively                   |
| Dense text blocks, no scan structure           | Every section: bold headline + 3-5 bullets + one visual anchor; never >60 chars/line                  |
| Multiple CTAs competing per spread             | One primary CTA per spread; secondary only if clearly subordinate (smaller, muted color)              |
| "90% of customers" social proof                | Use "9 out of 10" framing — Cialdini's research shows this connects more personally                   |
| Sending same collateral to all stakeholders    | Segment: technical buyer gets datasheet, economic buyer gets ROI one-pager, champion gets battle card |

## Checklist

- [ ] Collateral matches a specific buyer journey stage (not "general purpose")
- [ ] Single primary CTA per spread/page
- [ ] Benefits framed before features (outcome → mechanism, not mechanism → outcome)
- [ ] Social proof uses "X out of Y" framing, not percentages
- [ ] Strongest metric or outcome appears above the fold / on cover
- [ ] Layout follows Z-pattern (scan) or F-pattern (read) deliberately
- [ ] No more than 60% text coverage per page (minimum 40% whitespace + visuals)
- [ ] Brand-consistent colors, typography, and imagery across all pieces
- [ ] Persona-appropriate: C-suite gets different piece than technical evaluator
- [ ] Pricing only included if buyer is at decision stage or price is a competitive advantage
